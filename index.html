Section 1: Hosting. Displaying charts in my own website.

Chart 1 | The Relationship Between GDP and Life Expectancy by Region

This scatter plot visualizes the relationship between GDP per capita (on a log scale) and life expectancy across various regions of the world. Each dot represents a country, with the size of the dots corresponding to the population size. The countries are grouped by continent, as indicated by different colors: Africa (blue), America (orange), Asia (red), Europe (green), and Oceania (light blue). The plot suggests that higher GDP per capita is generally associated with longer life expectancy, with regional variations evident. Data sources include the UN Population Prospects 2022 for life expectancy and the World Bank for GDP.

Chart 2 | Declining Populations

This line graph illustrates the projected cumulative percentage change in population for several countries from 2020 to 2100. Countries like South Korea and Japan are expected to see significant population declines, while the UK and the US are projected to experience moderate population growth. Data source: UN.

Chart 3 | Gender Parity Deviation Across Asia-Pacific Countries

This horizontal bar chart displays the deviation from gender parity across various countries in the Asia-Pacific region. The bars extending to the left represent countries where gender parity has not been achieved, with Pakistan showing the highest deviation. The bars extending to the right indicate countries with a slight positive deviation, where more progress has been made toward gender parity. Countries like the Philippines and Solomon Islands exhibit a positive deviation toward gender balance, while countries like Tajikistan and Maldives show significant gender inequality. The chart emphasizes the varying degrees of gender parity across the region.

Chart 4 | Food-Related CO2 Greenhouse Gas Emissions

This stacked horizontal bar chart illustrates the amount of CO2 emissions (in kg) produced per kilogram of various types of food. The emissions are broken down by different stages of production and distribution: land use, farm activities, animal feed, processing, transport, retail, and packaging. The chart shows that beef (from both beef and dairy herds) and lamb & mutton generate the highest CO2 emissions, largely driven by land use and farm activities. On the other end, foods like peas, bananas, and maize have significantly lower emissions. The data source is "Our World in Data."

Section 2: Building. Creating my own visualisations

Chart 1 | Grain Imports. Ukraine

This line graph shows the sharp rise in grain imports to Ukraine from 2010 to 2023, with a particularly notable increase after 2020. The data highlights significant growth in recent years. The source for the data is UN COMTRADE through the ECO API.

Chart 2 | Vice Spending

This line chart tracks the spending trends on various "vice" products in the UK from 1998 to 2022, including alcohol (beer, spirits, wines, ciders), tobacco, and narcotics. The spending on tobacco shows a sharp increase, while other categories like narcotics and beer remain relatively steady. Data source: ONS (Office for National Statistics).

Section 3: Debating. Impact of Inflation on Living Standards

At the Festival of Economics 2024, experts discussed how inflation affects household purchasing power and overall living standards.

Chart 1 | Inflation Rate Over Time

This line chart shows the annual inflation rate from 2018 to 2023. The trend indicates a steady increase in inflation over recent years, with a notable spike in 2022. Rising inflation rates imply higher costs for goods and services, which can impact consumers’ purchasing power.

Chart 2 | Real Wages Over time

This line chart illustrates the average real wage from 2018 to 2023. The data shows a gradual decline in real wages, particularly from 2020 onwards, suggesting that wage growth has not kept pace with inflation. This decline can result in reduced purchasing power for households.

Section 4: Renewables - electricity generation

This bar chart displays the global annual electricity generation from renewable sources, measured in billion kilowatt-hours, from 1980 to 2020. The data shows a steady increase in renewable electricity generation over time, with a significant acceleration in growth starting around 2005. By 2020, renewable generation reached close to 9,000 billion kilowatt-hours, reflecting a major global shift towards renewable energy sources. The source of this data is the U.S. Energy Information Administration (US EIA).
